What I Do

My primary focus at NC State has been to improve predictions of storm surge and coastal flooding. These processes can be predicted by numerical models, including in real-time forecasts as a storm approaches the coast, to develop maps of inundation to support decision-making. However, these models can be slow, especially if they try to represent all the smallest flow pathways, like channels through a marsh, and barriers, like raised coastal highways. In my research, I am making it easier for models to represent flooding at those smallest scales, which will improve predictions at individual homes and critical infrastructure along our coasts.

To accomplish this research, I have become proficient in several coding languages, hydrodrynamic models, geospatial programs, and meshing software:

Projects

During my PhD I have been a lead contributor on two multi-institutional multi-disciplinary storm surge research projects.

Storm Surge Forecasting of the South Atlantic Bight | DHS CRCoE

For this project I developed and tested ocean-scale numerical meshes for use in storm surge modeling along the South Atlantic Bight. Ultimately, my aim is to develop an accurate, stable, and efficient mesh that can be used for real-time hurricane forecasting by utilizing mesh generation programs such as OceanMesh2D and the latest high-resolution bathymetric and topographic data.

Increasing the Accuracy and Efficiency of Storm Surge Models | NSF

Multi-institutional project that aims to improve the accuracy and efficiency of storm surge modeling by leveraging high-resolution bathymetric and land-cover data. This project required the theoretical development of subgrid correction factors in a continuous-Galerkin finite element framework and testing of corrections on ocean-scale storm surge models. Questions surrounding this project centered on the extent to which subgrid corrections could be levered in the ADvanced CIRCulation (ADCIRC) state-of-the-art hydrodynamic model.

Fun Research

Subjects I think would be fun to investigate.

Hurricanes on Mars

What if Mars had oceans? What if those oceans had tropical cyclones? How would the reduced gravity affect storm surge and wave propagation? Would the governing shallow water equations still be valid? These are fun questions to think about when trying to envision how water moves on different planets.

How can color affect the way we perceive data?

For far too long we have been relying on 'jet' colormaps to display storm surge and flooding results. So why do we use them? There has been ample research on how this rainbow colormap causes misperceptions in data communication. My goal long-term is to help educate fellow researchers on the effectiveness of using the proper colormap to display data. Tools like cmocean and SciVisColor from the Texas Advanced Computing Center are incredible resources that can really help improve scientific figure generation.

How do extreme wave events affect inland water levels on tropical islands?

Large storm systems stretching across hundreds of miles of open ocean during the winter months can cause massive wave events on tropical islands. Recently an enormous south pacific storm triggered Tahiti's second Code Red event which caused significant coastal flooding and forced the Tahitian government to ban boats and watercraft from leaving port. Waves at the Teahupo'o break were over 20 ft. What if this happened at a Pacific Naval port? How would Naval operations be affected? Photo: Pedro Bala
